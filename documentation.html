1560

Typographic style

To readapt the XVIth century typographic style we decided to reproduce on screen the style of a Cinquecentina. The term Cinquecentina it’s usually used to refer to the printed books of the XVIth century. Among this set of documents, a specific attention goes to those produced in Venice across the ‘500, since it’s estimated that in the city were printed half of the books circulating in Europe.

So, we took as a model the layout of a Venetian Cinquecentina, but we restyled it in order to adapt it to the unpaginated medium of nowadays browsers. The content appears on one justified column, large outer margins, and larger margins at the foot then at the head. However, in order to maintain the overflow of the text on the y-axis, typical of modern online publishing, the division between recto and verso of printed books was substituted by a one-column layout that resembles the act of reading a parchment.

Besides that, the proportions among the elements of the page (e.g. margins, font-sizes) were chosen by considering the classical rules of proportions, more specifically the so-called Golden Ratio (a ratio of 1.61). In addition, our layout reuses many elements of a XVth century Incunabula, such as thick borders and drop caps. By the way, XVIth century typographic style distinguishes itself from that of the XVth by a wider use of white spaces, large paddings, letter-spacing and line-height.

Fonts and Typographic elements

Since the most common typeface in the context of historical Venetian printed books was the lettera antiqua, due to its adoption by Aldo Manuzio, we decided to choose for our layouts Hultog, a simple "antiqued" font based on some classic Venetian-style typefaces, both normal and coursive. For Drop Caps we chose Goudy Initialen that resembles engraved floral initials of the time.

Since the wide use of illustration at the time, we decided to place images at the center and give them max-width, by also separating them from text by thick borders and large paddings. For the table we tried to reproduce the style in link and we used upper roman numbers for lists.

Cover

The cover presents a center-alignment of the text, with bibliographic information about the issue at the bottom and the Printer's mark on top of this, as it was used by printers like the famous Aldo Manuzio.

1860

1860 is a typographic style inspired by XIXth century newspaper and journals. The social, cultural and technological innovations of the century revolutionized printing press to the point of making journalism a market sustained by mass consumption and advertisement. With respect to the classical style of the printing press, in the second half of the XIXth century we assist to dramatical changes in the typographical choices, especially with respect to newspapers and periodicals. The sparse and harmonic disposition of types across the page, typical of the XVIth century, is substituted by a dense disposition of the text across multiple columns in the same page, with lesser line height and smaller margins. These stylistic choices were mainly due to the dramatic presence of advertisements, which occupied the majority of the pages of a journal.

Thus, we tried to mimic the typography of the second half of the 1800 by opting for small paddings, diminishing the line height and the font-size and displaying the text across a 3-column-layout. To evidence some parts of the text, like headings, we use condensed typefaces aligned centrally. For the paragraph we chose Bodoni, a serif font which is one of the most common modern typefaces. We applied borders and we made images and tables to spread across one single column, to not diminish the density of the page.

The cover presents our mark with a style that resembles XIXth century illustrations, and the image is placed under the title, with a large width, at the center of the cover. For the title of the issue we adopted “The Wanters”, a vintage typeface with quirky curved serif, perfectly designed for headers that give a XIXth century feel.
